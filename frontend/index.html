Overview

What this site does

CrisisLens aggregates news from 37+ sources across Arabic, Western, and independent media and uses natural language processing to surface moments where they directly contradict each other — different casualty figures, opposing framings of the same event, or one side's silence where another speaks loudly.

It does not tell you who is right. It shows you where the disagreement is.

This is a beta product built and maintained by one person. The pipeline makes automated editorial decisions that reasonable people would disagree with. Every number and threshold on this page is public so you can judge them yourself.
Sources & Trust Scores

Who we read and how much we weight them

Every source has a trust score between 0.10 and 1.00. Scores were assigned based on editorial independence, track record of corrections, funding transparency, and third-party ratings from AllSides, Ad Fontes Media, and Media Bias/Fact Check where available. They are not claims about accuracy — they are weights that influence how conflict scores are calculated.

Higher-trust pairs produce higher conflict scores. Two state-funded sources contradicting each other produce a lower score than a wire service contradicting a state broadcaster.

Pipeline

From raw headline to displayed conflict

Every 15 minutes the pipeline runs end-to-end automatically:

1
Ingest — RSS feeds and public Telegram channels are fetched. Articles are deduplicated by URL.
2
Translate — Arabic headlines and summaries are machine-translated to English using Google Translate (free tier). Bias analysis labels are also translated back to Arabic for the Arabic UI.
3
Embed — Each article is converted to a 384-dimensional semantic vector using paraphrase-multilingual-MiniLM-L12-v2, a local CPU model that handles Arabic and English natively. The embedding is built from headline + summary so topically similar but factually different articles are separated.
4
Pair — Articles with cosine similarity above 0.70 are paired as candidate contradictions. Same-source pairs are excluded. The pool is limited to recent articles (within 72 hours of each other) to avoid pairing stale reports.
5
NLI — Each pair is run through mDeBERTa-v3-base-xnli-multilingual-nli-2mil7, a zero-shot natural language inference model. It classifies the pair as entailment, neutral, or contradiction. Pairs scoring below the contradiction threshold are discarded.
6
Score — A conflict score is computed from the NLI contradiction probability, source trust weights, and a diversity bonus. The result is a single number from 0 to 1.
7
Framing analysis — A language model (Groq Llama 3) reads the article pair and writes a short prose analysis of the framing differences, emotional language, and named-party treatment between the two sources.
Conflict Scoring

How the conflict score is calculated

The conflict score shown on each card is computed as follows:

Base score = NLI contradiction probability × avg(trust_A, trust_B)
+ Numeric boost: +0.20 if the two articles contain conflicting numbers (casualties, distances, dates)
+ Framing boost: +0.15 if opposite framing vocabulary is detected (see Framing section)
+ Diversity bonus: +0.08 if the sources are from different region groups (e.g. Western vs Arab)
Threshold: pairs with a final score below 0.55 are not shown

The diversity bonus (+0.08) is the most contested decision in this pipeline. It means two sources from different editorial traditions get a slightly higher conflict score than two sources from the same tradition, even if the contradiction is identical in severity. The rationale: a contradiction between CNN and Al Jazeera is more editorially significant for CrisisLens's purpose (cross-narrative divergence) than a contradiction between two Western wire services covering the same story differently. You can disagree with this choice — it is explicit, not hidden.

Framing Detection

How we detect language framing differences

Framing detection looks for opposing vocabulary within the same article pair. If source A uses a word from one cluster and source B uses a word from the opposing cluster on the same event, a framing boost is applied to the conflict score.

The current vocabulary pairs are rule-based and were chosen to reflect documented framing differences in Middle East conflict journalism:

martyred / استشهد / شهيدvskilled / died
terrorist / إرهابيvsresistance / مقاوم / مجاهد
genocide / إبادةvsconflict / war / operation
occupation / احتلالvsdefense / security operation / self-defense
civilian / مدنيvsmilitant / combatant / fighter / مسلح

These are heuristics, not claims. A headline that uses "killed" is not more accurate than one that uses "martyred" — they reflect the editorial traditions and political positions of the publication. The framing boost does not penalise either side; it flags that a framing difference exists between the two sources in a pair.

The framing analysis prose (generated by an LLM on each conflict card) provides a more nuanced description of the specific framing differences in that pair.

Limitations

What this site cannot do

Translation quality. Arabic → English machine translation loses nuance. NLI runs on the translated text, not the original Arabic. A subtlety that disappears in translation is a contradiction we will miss.
NLI is not fact-checking. The model detects textual contradiction. Two articles can contradict each other and both be wrong. This is not a truth engine.
Trust scores are editorial judgments. They were assigned by one person based on available third-party data. They have not been independently validated. A source scored 0.40 may publish accurate reporting; a source scored 0.85 may have blind spots.
Coverage gaps. Sources that block the hosting server's IP (including some major Arabic outlets) are not included. The dataset skews toward sources with open RSS feeds and public Telegram channels.
LLM framing analysis. The prose framing analysis on each card is generated by Llama 3 (Groq). LLMs have documented political and cultural biases. The analysis is a starting point for reading, not a verdict.
This is a beta. Thresholds, weights, and source lists will change. The methodology page will be updated when they do.

Questions or disagreements with the methodology? Open an issue on GitHub.

Source Ethics

Why we include state-controlled and low-trust sources

CrisisLens includes sources that several governments have designated as state-controlled propaganda outlets — including RT Arabic (Russia), Press TV (Iran), and Tasnim News Agency (Iran). These sources carry trust weights of 0.35–0.40, the lowest in the dataset. We include them deliberately, and we want to explain why.

The contradiction between state media and independent reporting is itself the story. When RT Arabic and Reuters describe the same event in irreconcilable terms, that divergence is not noise to be filtered out — it is the signal CrisisLens exists to surface. Removing state media would eliminate the most editorially significant contradictions on the platform.

This is the same reasoning that drives quality journalism: the BBC, New York Times, and Al Jazeera all quote and report on state media output — not to endorse it, but because understanding what state-controlled outlets are claiming is essential context for readers trying to understand a conflict.

What the trust weight does — and does not do

A trust weight of 0.35 means a conflict pair involving that source receives a lower base score than one involving high-trust sources. State media articles are harder to surface as top conflicts when paired against each other. They surface prominently when paired against high-trust sources — precisely because that cross-narrative gap is what the platform is designed to show.

The trust weight does not suppress or hide the source. It does not label the source's content as false. It is a weighting factor in the scoring formula, not an editorial verdict on any individual article.

Transparency over curation

CrisisLens's position is that readers are better served by seeing the full spectrum of claims — with clear trust weights and source attribution — than by a curated feed that pre-filters which narratives are worth knowing about. Every source card shows the source name and links to the original article. Readers can evaluate the source themselves.

If you believe a source should be removed entirely, or that a trust weight is miscalibrated, open an issue on GitHub. The source list and weights are public and subject to revision.
